How to choose the right syringe filter

By the Dorsan Filtration team · Updated 4 June 2026 · 6 min read
Replace with: syringe-filter-hero.jpg (alt: "Range of coloured Dorsan syringe filters")

Choosing a syringe filter comes down to three decisions: the membrane material, the pore size, and the diameter. Get these right and you protect your instruments, your sample and your results. This guide walks through each choice in plain terms, with a quick-reference table you can use at the bench.

1. Pick the membrane material

The membrane is the heart of the filter. It must be compatible with your sample so it neither dissolves nor adsorbs the compounds you care about. Here is how the most common membranes compare.

MembraneBest forAvoid with
PVDF (hydrophilic)Aqueous and mild organic solvents, low protein bindingStrong acetone / DMSO
PTFE (hydrophobic)Aggressive solvents, acids, bases, gasesPure aqueous (needs pre-wet)
NylonGeneral aqueous & organic, robustProtein recovery (high binding)
PESFast aqueous flow, very low binding, biological samplesMany organic solvents
MCE / Cellulose acetateAqueous biological samples, low bindingOrganic solvents
Quick rule: aqueous biological sample → PES or cellulose acetate; mixed aqueous/organic → PVDF; harsh solvents → PTFE.

2. Choose the pore size

Pore size sets what the filter removes. Two sizes cover most laboratory work:

  • 0.45 µm — general clarification and particulate removal before analysis. The default for routine HPLC sample preparation.
  • 0.22 µm — sterilising-grade filtration to remove bacteria, and the choice for UHPLC or sensitive columns.

If your method does not specify, 0.45 µm protects most columns. Move to 0.22 µm when you need sterility or are running fine-particle-sensitive instrumentation.

3. Match the diameter to your volume

Diameter controls how much sample a filter can handle before it clogs:

  • 13 mm — small volumes up to ~10 mL. Minimises hold-up volume and sample loss.
  • 25 mm — volumes of 10–100 mL, or dirtier samples that would clog a smaller filter.

4. Check the details that trip people up

Two final points worth confirming before you order:

  1. Sterile vs non-sterile. Choose sterile, individually-packed filters for cell culture and sterility-critical work.
  2. Hold-up volume. For precious or low-volume samples, a smaller diameter recovers more of what you filter.

Frequently asked questions

Which syringe filter is best for HPLC?

For routine HPLC, a 0.45 µm PVDF or nylon filter works for most samples; switch to 0.22 µm PVDF for UHPLC or low-particle tolerance columns.

Can I reuse a syringe filter?

No. Syringe filters are single-use; reusing one risks carry-over contamination and inaccurate results.

Do I need to pre-wet a PTFE filter?

For aqueous samples, yes — hydrophobic PTFE needs wetting with a little alcohol first, or choose a hydrophilic membrane instead.
